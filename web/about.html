Why ORQL Exists

"Most systems we obey are just consensus hallucinations. They feel real because we all act as if they are."

I am guessing that much like AdS/CFT we might be in a singularity hologram that contains a 1 and 0 and everything else is a sequencing of the rest in all sorts of ways that keeps expanding with entropy like a blossoming flower branching into infinity. What I am saying probably answers and raises millions of questions but my goal is to simply push the needle into a jailbreak of the classical modes of thinking. I am probably mostly wrong and I accept/embrace that whole heartedly as a part of the unfolding.

Why I Built ORQL

ORQL was born from existing quantum theories ignoring the observer as a formal entity, and that felt like a foundational error. Quantum mechanics treats the observer as something external, undefined, or tacked on after the math. But in reality, every measurement, every collapse, every "world" happens from the perspective of an observer embedded in the system.

Why ORQL Had to Exist:

  • I was dissatisfied with current interpretations of the wavefunction collapse as they felt incomplete. Copenhagen's vagueness and MWI's total abstraction.
  • I needed a system where collapse is not universal, but local to the observer's information state.
  • I wanted to describe how different observers in the same system can diverge without contradiction.
  • I am willing to break with orthodoxy - but not logic - to get there.
ORQL is a process shaped by memory, entanglement, and informational context. ORQL exists to add observers to the quantum equation ‚Äî to formalize subjective collapse, divergence, and meaning without sacrificing logic. It's a new language for a reality that's always been relational.

Core Beliefs

Reality is Observer-Dependent

Everything probably exists but not everything that exists exists for everyone. Collapse is not a universal event, but a localized transition.

Open Science

Knowledge shouldn't be gated by institutions despite the full acknowledgement that they have served incredible progress to fields of science. I believe in democratizing access to frameworks, simulations, and conceptual models.

Independent Thought

I deeply respect academia while acknowledging some of the most important insights come from outsiders. (Think: Ramanujan's notebooks, Einstein's patent office papers, Barbara McClintock's genetics work)

Epistemic Honesty > Consensus

I'd rather say "we don't know" than pretend an illusion is true. This pursuit is about getting closer to what's real, even if that means admitting most of what we "know" might be wrong.

Guiding Principles
